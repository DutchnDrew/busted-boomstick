Broad Strokes

Thriller | Short Story by Drew Eveland

"Well, Ho. Lee. Shit. Is this a prank call, cuz I'm pretty sure the guy who used to own this number is dead," Paul said into his cell phone as he pulled into his suburban Virginia driveway.

The voice on the phone laughed. "Not dead, just married now. The two are close maybe and I could see how it would confuse a dumbass like you."

"Jesus, Tommy," Paul started and was interrupted.

"I know, I know. It has been quite a while. Too long. Sorry, I haven't called," Tommy replied.

"Bah, don't sweat it," Paul said. "You think it's bad now? Wait til she pops out a couple crotch-goblins. Then you're in for it. Tell you what, social distancing is fucking easy when you no longer know anyone who lives outside your house. You just wait. Kids are awesome, but I can tell you from experience, any remnant of a social life you might still have will be friggin extinct. Dead as a damn dinosaur, Tommy… which is alright, cuz no one ever fucking liked you anyway."

"Yeah, yeah, yeah," Tommy came back. "How are your little shit-stains doing these days? Hopefully they don't look like you yet."

It was Paul's turn to chuckle. "Hopefully, they never will."

Tommy said quickly, "five and two, now?"

"Six and three. Junior just started kindergarten. Kiera…," Paul paused momentarily and thought. "You've never met Kiera, have you?"

"No, I haven't. I need to. It's been way too long, which is stupid considering you're just across the river," Tommy said.

Tommy was right, but on the other hand, they were both busy young men, Paul with a wife and two young children, and Tommy with a new wife in the honeymoon of their marriage. Plus, no distance that required an automobile for travel in the Washington beltway could be considered "short".

"So, how are you?" Paul asked. "You wearing a nice pink tie today?"

"It's cornflower blue, asshole," Tommy snapped back, continuing the game. The tie exchange had been part of every conversation the two had had for a few years now, ever since Tom left the Army for a high-speed counter-terrorism position in the National Security Agency. Tom and Paul had met in Iraq, Paul already several years into a career in the Central Intelligence Agency, and Tom a Green Beret, Army Special Forces. The best of the best. Paul knew damn well that Tom's NSA position was in operations and every bit as high speed as what he had done in the Army, more so, probably, but that only made Paul want to paint him as a desk-riding bureaucrat even more. The kind of guy who wears pink ties to work and sends his coworkers memos about nonsense that no one with a single spark of life or single working brain cell would care about.

"But, actually," Tommy continued, "I am calling you about work."

Now sitting in his car in his driveway, Paul's face bunched up in curiosity, the wheels starting to turn in a more serious direction. "Alright, what's up?" Paul's voice became cautious and the walls of his mind began to raise. While unofficial work-talk between colleagues was how the best work was usually accomplished in the intelligence world, it was always approached cautiously and conscientiously, especially when done outside of a secure facility, and as a rule, never over the phone or internet.

"Well, first," Tommy paused, "I don't want to waste your time. Are you still working for the clowns? The Ouiji board crew?" Paul called the CIA, Clowns in Action. In fact, that was a pretty common joke in the intel community. As for the Ouiji board bit, Paul was uncomfortable that he even made that allusion on the phone.

"Yes, I do." After returning from Iraq, a combination of Paul's big brain and either good or bad luck swept him into the more outlandish corners of the agency, skunkworks type stuff, the kind of projects that end up in history books after a Japanese city of 80,000 is obliterated or are finally revealed with embarrassment a generation later. Turns out time-travel was not possible, and spending big money to try and make it happen makes you look like the regurgitated contents of a clown car.

The 'Ouiji board crew' as Tommy called it was Project Arcanos, a working group that dealt with what was called on the fringes of intelligence, 'remote viewing'. In plain terms, remote viewers were psychics. You could ask them questions about where an important person was, or ask them to locate a downed aircraft, and they would draw pictures of what they saw or go into a trance and start writing down coordinates. It sounded like science fiction, bad science fiction, but the people who worked in those rooms did not doubt that there was something factual, something real, behind their abilities. They were successful too often for any doubt to exist. Still, for all their successes there were plenty of failures. While no one doubted there was something real there, no one actually understood it and that left plenty of room for fuck ups and so for every remote viewer in the program there were at least four handlers devoted to various tasks surrounding the people and the process, from debunking, facilitating, clarifying, or scouting for new talent. Whatever could be done to strengthen a viewer's connection to his target or translate what he or she came up with to the world outside their suite of rooms was the job of the handlers. And on the other side of the coin, whatever could be done to debunk the fraudulent or crazy and separate them from CIA operations was also handler responsibility. Paul was a handler.

"Do you think we could meet up?" Tommy asked. "One of our houses?"

"Yeah." Paul replied, "Your place might be better… no kids."

"Good. Cheri," Tommy said, referencing his wife, "won't be in til late tomorrow. Do you think you could make it over by five or six?"

"Yeah," Paul said. "Call it six. Can you text me your address again?"

"Sure thing. Thank you, Paul."

"No problem," Paul replied. They said their good byes and Paul pushed the end call button on his phone. He scanned his garage door, from left to right, top to bottom, as if he were looking for something. Something wasn't right. That 'thank you' was too heavy, too sincere. Something wasn't right.

***

"Hey shit heel," Paul called out, shutting the car door outside a suburban Reston condo.

"Nasty, dirty, leg," Tommy replied, coming out his door with a Cheshire cat grin.

The two men performed the handshake-hug of genuine affection with masculine reservations then went inside.

"So, what's going on, man? What's the deal," Paul asked.

"I've already brought it up," Tommy replied. "Come in here."

Tommy walked from his small, fresh-out-of-the-package living room into the open dining room and sat at the table behind large, black, laptop, wiggling the mouse to wake the computer.

Paul pulled up a second chair next to his friend and sat down. Paul looked at the computer and saw a web browser with three or four active tabs. The first one was appeared to be a selection of paintings for sale on Ebay.

Tommy made a 'bear with me gesture' with one hand. "Okay, let me actually skip ahead to what I saw after I decided to start digging. Then I'll go back to why I started," Tommy said.

Tommy clicked on a link for a painting being sold. It was pretty well made and lifelike, although rather devoid of any feeling. It was a painting of a plane crashing into what was clearly recognizable as the old World Trade Center. It was the second plane, on the other side of the painting, the first tower was already bleeding smoke into the sky.

Tommy made eye-contact with Paul for a moment. "This was an old sale posting, dated the fourth of September, 2001," he said, pointing to a date in fine type underneath the painting.

The look on Paul's face showed he clearly did not see where this was going but invited Tommy to continue.

"Assuming the date on here is correct, he posted this painting a week before the 9/11 attacks. If you click on it and look at the comments section, you can see the floodgates of internet shit talk open. Everyone roasts him for his shameless exploitation, saying he has no respect, is un-American, should be shot, blah blah blah, the typical keyboard commander shit. He tries to reply that it isn't the World Trade Center, that he painted this thing weeks before the attack and it has nothing to do with what just happened and he'd take it down. Which he did."

"Okay," Paul said. "Now, I know you have more than this. Give me the hook."

Tommy nodded. "Here. This is what I saw, totally by happenstance two days ago." Tommy opened up the second tab on the browser. Two click clacks later and another image showed up on the screen.

Another painting with an explosion. This was no sky scraper, though; it struck Paul as being foreign, Middle Eastern, more precisely. It was a large building, two stories of dark sandstone with a small outbuilding on top of the flat roof, called in the region, a baytuna.

"This guy has an explosion fetish or something?" Paul asked.

Tommy nodded. "His name is Pious Sinclaire, and yes… all of them. Every. Single. One. 72 in all that I have found from him on a couple of different sites, going back to 2001. He's actually local too, more or less. Lives in North Carolina."

Paul looked at him quizzically, "Are we building to point?" He put ironic air quotes of emphasis on the word building.

"Yup," Tommy replied quickly. "Okay, just a couple quick things I want you to notice. In the distance," Tommy pointed at three distant buildings on the arid, barren landscape of the painting. "These three buildings."

Paul nodded.

"Red rose bushes, three orange trees, and the walkway from the front porch area out past the garden." Tommy pointed quickly to the irrelevant details. "And last, these are pretty cool, actually," Tommy said pointing to the front of the house, "the columns."

The columns, two large and stately sandstone columns stood visible going from the front patio up to a balcony extending from the flat roof, generally visible just beyond the edges of the detonation near the rear, right corner of the building. The columns were carved with alternating hands and snakes, circling up the entire column in the candy cane pattern of an old barbershop pole. Each snake's mouth was open, a forearm and hand extending from inside, the fist holding on to the body of the next snake. The pattern repeated up the length of both columns.

"So, you are getting those pillars installed in front of your condo?" Paul asked sarcastically. "I mean, they are pretty damn cool, but I don't think your HOA would appreciate it."

"Har har har," Tommy replied. "Paul. This house exists. I know the place. It's in Yemen and it's being used as a logistics hub for Al-Qaeda's external operations. We've had twenty-four-seven eyes on it for months now." He paused to make sure Paul was really listening. "We are planning on hitting it within the next few days."

Paul grunted an acknowledgment, suddenly quite serious. "This couldn't be a coincidence?" Paul asked.

"No," Tommy replied. "I've been staring at this place for weeks. The details here are all precise and correct. Right down to the location of each plant and each crooked paving stone. This IS the place."

***

Paul drove home unbothered by the beltway traffic. The stop and go barely registered and his mind played through the previous three hours. After digesting what Tommy had told him, Paul had painstakingly gone through several sites finding and printing off a copy of every explosion-riddled painting, 72 in all, posted online by Mr. Pious Sinclaire. Those copies now sat in a manila folder in the passenger seat of Paul's Honda.

Paul would pursue this. He trusted Tommy's word implicitly. They had put their lives in each other's hands back in the sandbox and they knew each other's mettle, but Paul had to take another step in research before he brought this to official channels. He knew that if he were convinced, he had enough clout in his small, outlandish, corner of the CIA, to push any action he deemed appropriate and have it done quickly.

Stepping into his house, he found his wife sitting in the living room. "Hi, love. Thanks for handling the kids tonight," Paul said, hanging his jacket on the coatrack beside the door.

"No biggie," she replied. "You working tonight?" She asked, in the untaught, but well-understood coded language of spouses in the intelligence industry. What the question was really asking was, "Are there things you need to do which require you to be left alone and unquestioned?"

"Yeah," Paul replied. "I expect I'll be up for a while."

"Here or do you have to go into the building?" she inquired.

"Just here, for the moment." Paul walked towards a room that functioned as a study-slash-office as needed.

"You want something to eat?" She called to him from the other room.

Paul smiled happily. "I would love something."

***

Paul drove to work early the next morning with action on his mind. The night before, the analyst in him had gone to work, looking up on the internet notable terrorist attacks which had occurred over the last ten years. He thanked the stars for Wikipedia, THE place for people who did not need to site sources but needed access a lot of information and quickly. He looked for pictures of the targets, buildings mostly, that had been damaged or destroyed in these attacks and compared them to his stack of 72 paintings. He did the same with several US and Israeli strikes against terrorist compounds of interest, although those incidents were much more difficult to find on low-side, and finding pictures of any use even more difficult.

Still, Paul's amateurish sleuthing found four solid matches: the suicide car bombing of the British embassy in Mombassa, the Rocket attack on the US embassy in Baghdad, the bombing of a popular night club in Berlin, and the American strike on Abu Ayub Al-Misri's hideout in the Pakistani tribal area. Paul wished he'd been able to find more, but with resources and methodology as limited as they were the night before, he was happy with what he'd found and what he'd found led him to believe that there was more there. He was sure of it.

What had to be done now was bring the issue into the official domain of the CIA, brief the necessary parties concerning the situation and request the authority to fast-track an investigation into Pious Sinclaire to gather more information that would help them determine their next plan of action. Paul had no doubts about his ability to make this all happen. His department was small and isolated, away from the flagpole, was the phrase, and Paul commanded the respect of those with the authority to get what he wanted. He had no doubts.

It went as predicted over the next two days. Paul was able to sit with the office director, Mark Ogglethorp for half an hour that first morning and the next day, they were able to start the processing of a security investigation into Pious Sinclaire. Mark had the paperwork sent over from the appropriate office. Paul spent the afternoon filling it out and Mark made one or two calls to make sure that when everything was filed, it would be moved on without delay.

Three days later, he had received approval from corporate to make contact with Pious Sinclaire. This basic approval meant that at this point only the most rudimentary security screening on Sinclaire had been completed, but that had not yielded any information relating to either criminal or foreign intelligence activity.

Paul called him the same day. Contrary to popular imagery, CIA agents were not always clandestine. When they pitched their assets, they typically did not hide their affiliation or beat around the bush regarding what they wanted from a person. The most subterfuge typically involved was the use of a pseudonym, but even that was reserved for field agents, which Paul was not. In that initial phone conversation, Paul was vague, but not misleading. Initially, he only told Mr. Sinclaire that he worked for the government, they had seen his art work, he was not in any trouble, and the US government was requesting his help. They agreed to meet at Mr. Sinclaire's residence that Wednesday.

Mr. Sinclaire's home was a small condominium in a tightly packed row of postage stamp lots.

Paul parked half a block away and walked the short distance, taking in his surroundings automatically. It was a quiet, suburban neighborhood, the sort of place where people mowed their lawns at exacting intervals and HOA newsletters were taken seriously. Paul knocked once, firmly. The door opened almost immediately.

Pious Sinclaire was younger than Paul expected. Mid-thirties, maybe. Tall, lean, and neat. He looked like a man who took care of himself. No jewelry, no piercings, nothing ostentatious. Just an air of quiet confidence.

"Mr. Sinclaire?" Paul asked.

"Yes," he replied, stepping aside. "You must be Paul."

Paul entered, scanning the small space. It was clean and minimal, with several large, framed canvases leaning against the walls. The newest works, Paul assumed.

"Thanks for agreeing to meet," Paul began.

"Of course. When someone from the government calls, you listen."

The next hour passed in polite conversation. Paul explained the CIA's interest in his paintings, hinting that his "talent" might be useful to national security interests. Pious nodded with understanding and humility. He never once claimed to be psychic, but he didn't deny it either.

Paul left satisfied. He wasn't completely sold, but he had enough to justify bringing Pious in for an orientation at Langley—an opportunity to observe him under more controlled conditions.

The next few days were a grind of paperwork, emails, and hallway conversations. Paul compiled every piece of footage, each media mention, and the transcripts of their interviews into a tight packet. He knew it wasn't conclusive, but it was compelling enough to get a foot in the door.

His first meeting was with Deputy Director Harkness, a practical man who didn't waste time on theatrics. Paul laid it all out—Pious' improbable accuracy, his unusual composure, and the unsettling way he anticipated classified strikes. Harkness didn't nod, didn't frown. He only asked one question: "Is he the real deal or part of something else?"

Paul answered the only way he could: "That's what I want to find out. But if he's faking it, he's doing it better than anyone I've ever seen."

There was a pause, then a quiet, reluctant nod. "Bring him in. Keep him close."

It took another two days for security processing. Paul vouched for him personally, which earned more than a few sideways glances. Visitor access was granted—strictly limited, of course—but it was enough.

The following week, Pious walked through CIA security with a visitor badge clipped to his lapel. Paul met him at the entrance and led him down corridors humming with fluorescent light. They spoke of process, of procedure, of possibility. Pious took it all in with a measured calm.

"We'll head into the Arcanos wing first," Paul said, gesturing left. "Get you familiar with the space. Tomorrow, we'll run a baseline test. Just to see what you can do."

"Of course," Pious said. "It's an honor."

As Paul keyed the entry into the Arcanos suite, the doors unlocked with a pneumatic hiss. Pious stepped forward.

"Paul," he said.

Paul turned, brow furrowing. "Yeah?"

"Thank you," Pious said.

And then he closed his eyes.

He closed his eyes and thought of the friends he lost. He closed his eyes and thought of the buildings he was presented to paint just before their importance was leaked to American intelligence. He thought of all the sacrifices that were made, this elaborate trail of breadcrumbs in a trail leading to credibility, to attention, to invitation. All made so this thoughtful strategist, this brilliant artist could go through the final motion of falling forward and catching himself, this simple act of walking.

The explosion tore through three floors of CIA headquarters. Shards of shrapnel punched through walls and bodies alike. A fireball lit the hallway. Sirens screamed. Chaos reigned.

In the aftermath, as black smoke curled into the Virginia sky, officials scrambled to piece together what had happened. How a suicide bomber had walked straight into Langley. How an artist had fooled the most advanced intelligence agency in the world.

And how, in a way, Pious Sinclaire had painted his final masterpiece.
