Broad Strokes

Thriller | Short Story by Drew Eveland

"Well, Ho. Lee. Shit. Is this a prank call, cuz I'm pretty sure the guy who used to own this number is dead," Paul said into his cell phone as he pulled into his suburban Virginia driveway.

The voice on the phone laughed. "Not dead, just married now. The two are close maybe and I could see how it would confuse a dumbass like you."

"Jesus, Tommy," Paul started and was interrupted.

"I know, I know. It has been quite a while. Too long. Sorry, I haven't called," Tommy replied.

"Bah, don't sweat it," Paul said. "You think it's bad now? Wait til she pops out a couple crotch-goblins. Then you're in for it. Tell you what, social distancing is fucking easy when you no longer know anyone who lives outside your house..."

[Full story continues with Paul's reunion with Tommy, an NSA operative, who reveals he's discovered something disturbing: an artist named Pious Sinclaire whose paintings of explosions have an uncanny ability to predict terrorist attacks before they happen.]

Paul, a CIA handler for a remote viewing program, investigates Sinclaire's 72 paintings, finding four solid matches to actual attacks. He arranges a meeting with the artist, bringing him into CIA headquarters for testing.

What Paul doesn't realize is that Sinclaire has spent years creating this elaborate trail of breadcrumbs—sacrificing friends, leaking intelligence, painting destruction—all for this moment. When Pious closes his eyes in the Arcanos wing of CIA headquarters, he doesn't remote view.

He detonates.

In the aftermath, as black smoke curls into the Virginia sky, officials scramble to understand how a suicide bomber walked straight into Langley. How an artist fooled the most advanced intelligence agency in the world.

And how, in a way, Pious Sinclaire painted his final masterpiece.
