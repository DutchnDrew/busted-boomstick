Death God

Dark Fantasy | Short Story by Drew Eveland

Japanese mythology holds that some weapons, some blades have a killing spirit of their own; they desire to leap out of the scabbard and spill blood.

It has been written that a god is born when it gains its first worshipper and dies when its last altar falls to dust.

...and when a killing sword becomes a god?

The story follows Ngu Cho, a dentist in Seoul who lives a life of meticulous conformity—perfect clothes, perfect apartment, perfect routine. At 38, he realizes he will never marry, never meet anyone, never escape the crushing weight of maintaining appearances.

One Saturday evening, after purchasing a new sport coat to avoid potential criticism, Ngu returns home, steps to his window, and falls.

But this is only the beginning. The narrative weaves through three interconnected lives:

Nicholas Petrov — A Soviet mine manager who discovers that forced labor is more "humane" and efficient than building new infrastructure. His quarterly production reviews show record highs as he transforms prisoners into disposable assets.

Jason Kessler — A Compliance Outreach Director in a dystopian American future, where Patriot Scores and National Worship Days maintain order. He visits non-compliant families with warm words and cold certainty, his empathy slowly extinguished by the system he serves.

Three lives. Three eras. One thread: the transformation of human beings into instruments of death—whether by their own hand, economic calculus, or ideological machinery.

Death God is a meditation on precision, power, and the cost of perfection. On how systems shape souls, and how even the most meticulous conformity cannot protect us from becoming what we serve.
