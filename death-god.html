Death God

Dark Fantasy | Short Story by Drew Eveland

Japanese mythology holds that some weapons, some blades have a killing spirit of their own; they desire to leap out of the scabbard and spill blood.

It has been written that a god is born when it gains its first worshipper and dies when its last altar falls to dust.

…and when a killing sword becomes a god?

Ngu Cho

Ngu came home to his small, one-bedroom apartment above the Gangnam district of Seoul. It was 9 PM. He was exhausted. He removed his cap, the latest style from Banana Republic, which had cost him the equivalent of 68 dollars, and placed it on the pinnacle of his coat rack. He hung up his long, matching gray wool trench coat, an entirely respectable ensemble that nicely complimented his wool trousers, white collared shirt and black tie, which was tied in a tight double Windsor knot. No one could say anything negative about his wardrobe; there could be no negative comment from the neighbors. Always clean, always conservative, always a picture to the world that his little piece of the universe was in perfect order. That universe was always a little better than those that surrounded him, as it was meant to be, but never gaudily so, never directly stating its superiority, only implying it. Matching the neighbors, keeping pace with the neighbors, but always, with such diligent effort, being half a step ahead of the neighbors.

Perhaps he was wrong though. He did not wear a sports coat over his dress shirt, as a businessman might. It did not make sense; Ngu was not a businessman; he was a dentist and as soon as he arrived at the office in the morning, he would don a clean, glowing white lab coat. But that missing piece of uniform might leave him open to comment. Did someone on the subway car notice that there was no sportscoat lapel visible beneath his trench when it opened? Ngu hoped not and told himself that this would need to be remedied. He would take the ration of money he had set aside for a night out this weekend and buy the sportscoat instead.

Ngu removed his impeccable black leather loafers and walked into the apartment. A clean black sofa dominated the room, buttressing one wall, staring over the small glass coffee table to the modest flat screen television mounted on the opposite wall. His pride, though, was straight in front of him. A series of three large windows looked down over the city. Many people had larger apartments, but his tiny apartment had windows, and his apartment was in the correct part of the city.

The rent cost him three-quarters of his income, and his meals suffered for it. His entertainment suffered for it, not that he had much time for entertainment. Ngu worked sixty hours a week, often more. It was important to work. One did not want to be called lazy. But he also needed that money to pay the rent. To conform. To stay with the pack, but always just half a step ahead. He would never meet a girl. He would never marry. But he would never be exposed to negative comment. He would never bring that shame. He was 38.

Ngu boiled a pot of noodles on the tiny two-burner stovetop in the corner of his studio apartment that served as the kitchen and slurped them with soya while watching a gameshow on his television. It was a good television, but it could be bigger. If anyone ever visited, they might think that it should be bigger. Ngu went to bed after dinner so he could be ready for work again the next day.

The following Saturday, Ngu returned home from work at the same 9 PM hour. This time, though, he only removed his cap and his heavy wool trench coat. He kept his new sports coat on and did not remove his shoes. Smoothly, without a moment of hesitation, second thought, or twitch of conflict on his stoic face, Ngu crossed his one room apartment, slid open one of his large, perfect windows, and took one more step outside, plummeting to the street below. He lost one shoe on the way down.

Nicholas Petrov

Nicholas Petrov leaned over the large table, scanning the maps of the Karloff sulfide mine which covered it like a table cloth. His dark blond hair was lightly oiled, cropped short, and parted neatly on the right side. He had gaunt cheeks, a sharp chin, and hard brown eyes. Nicholas resembled a bird of prey, a raptor, and the lean but powerful man wore the tan shirt and trousers of a factory manager or foreman. At thirty-five, Nicholas operated the Karloff sulfide mine, managed 800 patriotic workers, and was responsible for producing one ton of nickel, four tons of tin, and eight tons of copper every cycle, as well as disposing of one hundred times that amount of slag. At thirty-five, Nicholas Petrov was absolutely a predator.

A predator yes, but malicious or vindictive, no. Nicholas bore no ill will to any man, not those who worked for him, not to those party members who may see him as a competitor, nor to his superiors above him. Nicholas had one goal, one motivation, was fixated on only one thing. As he soared over the people, the plans, the deep shafts and tunnels of the mine itself, his eyes always held steady on the goals of the next five-year plan. Those numbers, quotas, targets, production yields, efficiency rates, overhead and labor costs, were a never-ending, ever-changing spreadsheet running through his mind.

He focused on a map showing a long horizontal tunnel running North-East from one of the deepest levels of the central mine shaft. It extended over three miles out, with many old branches along the way, but at the extend of that long, singular shoot burst an impressive honeycomb of tunnels. Late last year, his excavators discovered a rich vein of nickel and cadmium and as exploratory tunnels expanded, so did the range and richness of the deposits.

Most tunnels and side branches did not extend more than a mile from a vertical shaft. Transporting raw earth was a labor intensive and time-consuming endeavor, so keeping the distances short was an imperative for efficient work. Petrov considered that this may be the appropriate time to drill a secondary shaft, even move the main hub of surface operations to a location above this new deposit. This chrome and steel falcon of industrial production knew he could get party approval for the operation given the richness of the new site and the faith and trust regional party leadership placed in him. Petrov was an intelligent, fervent, communist, and they viewed him as one of the future leaders in the war of Marxist expansion and Soviet survival. He had both the economic and production arguments on his side as well as the support of leadership to make it happen.

The effort would take a year, half a year if forced labor was employed. A small town, Rittlenburg, may need to be entirely relocated. A German name, Petrov thought. After relocation, maybe it could be given a better one. New roads built; forests cleared. A massive influx of excavators, drills, trucks, and human capital, rail lines laid, carts, earth moving machinery. It would be difficult and costly work, very intense for the year or six months as the new infrastructure was put in place. But after that, Petrov thought, much more efficient. Production costs would lower and eventually make up for the initial investment. While the work would be grueling for a year, the long-term costs of human capital would diminish. Mining took its toll on a human body. Cramped working conditions, poorly equipped medical facilities, the constant inhalation of dust and chemical fumes, the hard labor of breaking and hauling earth. Digging a new shaft would be more humane. More humane.

Digging a new shaft would be more humane.

More humane.

That phrase stuck like a skipping record in Petrov's skull and suddenly he couldn't think. The creation of the new shaft and new aboveground operation center could be achieved within six months if forced labor was utilized. Forced labor, plus investment, machinery, the movement of a town. But more efficient over the long term. Better production over the long term.

Forced labor comes from the gulags, Petrov thought, from the prisons. Petrov bore them no ill-will. Some people just needed education. But that which creates production is an asset. That which consumes production is a liability. Prisoners are a liability. And with that thought, the solution dawned on him. There would be no new shaft or new camp and facilities. No town would be moved. Efficient production could be created through other means.

Two months later Nicholas Petrov looked through the windows of his office overlooking the entrance to the mine shaft. Lines of hard, gaunt men hauled heavy ropes, pulling earth-laden carts on iron rails towards the initial sifting site. Several of this crew looked ready to drop. Petrov was unconcerned. A new crew of 64 was due to arrive from the Tblisi-Gordov prison in the next few days.

Petrov looked at his quarterly production reviews. Tonnage of earth moved, ore oxidized, and purified metals shipped were at an all-time high. His food costs were slightly higher, but his medical costs were lower. The dangerous, physical jobs were now performed almost entirely by the conscripts, and they rarely received medical treatment. Petrov took the food and housing costs that the Union's prisons would have normally logged for inmate housing and sustenance, and recorded them as assets, as he had saved the system that much money. The model was being looked at for adoption throughout the country, for which he was proud. The prisons were emptying and liabilities were becoming assets. Petrov was far ahead of his five-year plan.

Jason Kessler

The sun rose red over a checkpoint marked with two flags—one the familiar stars and stripes, now with gold leaf trim, and the other a noble eagle clenching a crucifix in its talons. A billboard behind it read, "One Nation. One People. One Truth." Cameras scanned each passing vehicle, and military police in black uniforms with gold trim waved commuters through with sleek, polymer-stock rifles slung across their backs.

Jason Kessler adjusted his collar as he stepped out of his electric sedan. The car was polished, American-made, and tagged with a barcode visible to any federal drone flying overhead. Jason, 47, had been promoted last quarter to Vice Regional Director of Compliance Outreach for the Southeastern Patriot Zone, a title that filled a business card and guaranteed him early retirement at 55—should he earn a clean Patriot Score each quarter until then.

Jason wore the required attire: a navy-blue jacket with a small, embroidered flag above the breast, khaki slacks, and polished brown shoes. His shirt, crisp white, was newly pressed, and his lapel displayed two loyalty pins—one for service, one for ideological clarity. A third pin, blank, awaited assignment, but Jason dared not guess at the meaning of the blank space.

He passed through six biometric scanners before reaching his office on the 32nd floor. Inside, glass walls offered a view of the perimeter tower—crowned with a surveillance dome overlooking the Liberty Corridor, formerly Martin Luther King Jr. Boulevard. Jason poured a glass of zero-sugar tea and settled behind his desk. The report was already waiting for him.

The non-compliant family in question lived 24 miles outside the Zone, in a gated community once known for its public libraries and independent bookstores. That had changed after the Truth Standardization Act of 2049. Jason's job was not merely to enforce law, but to educate. To root out moral decay. To protect national unity by guiding citizens back to the path.

He clicked open the family dossier. Non-participation in National Worship Day. Refusal to recite the Loyalty Oath. Suspicion of consuming unvetted news streams. Their two children had not been registered for the revised Civics Curriculum. The father's Patriot Score had dropped below 2.7.

Jason let out a quiet sigh. He would have to schedule a Compliance Encouragement Visit.

The visit occurred three days later. A convoy of three black vans arrived outside the residence. Jason stepped out first, flanked by two agents in light gray suits. The father met them at the door, face pale, hands trembling. Jason asked the man to confirm his identity, then extended a hand with the warmth of a weathered politician.

"You understand why we're here," Jason said. "This isn't punishment. This is your opportunity to rejoin us. To stand with your people."

The man broke into tears. He begged. Apologized. Swore he would recite the Oath, enroll the children, attend the rallies. Jason nodded. Promises were common.

The father was taken into a van to complete the Realignment Seminar. Jason remained with the wife and children, assuring them everything would be alright. He offered a card with a holographic eagle embossed on the front. "For Counseling and Reintegration Services," he said.

That night, Jason returned to his high-rise apartment. He made himself a plate of synthetic chicken, watched a brief on the growing threat of "Ideological Decay," and plugged in his brainwave monitor to complete his evening check-in. The machine chirped softly as it read his vitals, moods, and fluctuations. Jason's readings were normal. Slight spike in cortisol. Minor empathetic echo. Nothing actionable.

He showered. Dried. Stared at himself in the mirror. Three pins now gleamed on his lapel.

The next morning, the news broadcast showed a family voluntarily relocating to a Loyalty Restoration Center. Jason recognized the faces. A caption scrolled across the screen: "Together, we are indivisible."

Jason watched without expression. Behind him, through his 32nd floor window, a white drone passed silently across the red dawn sky.
