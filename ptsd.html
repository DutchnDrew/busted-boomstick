Self-Imposed

Self-Imposed is a meditation on the invisible prisons we build for ourselves—born not of external forces, but of guilt, grief, trauma, and the harsh self-judgments we quietly carry. In this piece, I explore the tension between internal suffering and outward stillness—the way a person can appear composed while being emotionally caged just beneath the surface.

The subject is isolated, not by walls, but by weight—emotional, psychological, maybe even spiritual. The title speaks to the cruel paradox of healing: that often, the hardest chains to break are the ones we've locked ourselves in. There is no captor here, no enemy. Just the quiet echo of one's own voice whispering doubts, replaying regrets, and resisting forgiveness.

With a muted palette punctuated by sharp contrasts, Self-Imposed draws the viewer into a space that is at once desolate and deeply human. Through each brushstroke and layered detail, I tried to express the ongoing tension between the desire for freedom and the fear of what that freedom might reveal.

Ultimately, Self-Imposed is not just about suffering—it's about the possibility of stepping out of it. It doesn't offer answers, but it asks the questions I've wrestled with myself: What am I still holding onto? And what would it take to let it go?

The Artist's Statement

From the book:

These aren't easy pieces to look at, and they weren't easy to create. But they needed to exist. Every piece here comes from a specific memory, a specific moment. Some are from deployments. Some are from the quiet afterward, when the noise stops and the ghosts come out. They're not pretty, and they're not meant to be.

If you're here because you know that weight, you're not alone. That's the whole point.
